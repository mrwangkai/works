Remitly UX Design

Recipient Quick Send

Streamlined repeat transfer experience for Remitly users, enabling faster transactions to saved recipients

Overview

Send shortcuts make it easier for customers to quickly send transfers. Currently Remitly had one send shortcut: the "send again" button on completed transfer cards. However, this shortcut was modeled on a transfer-first mindset, while research and analysis showed that Remitly users actually have a recipient-first mindset.

The Recipient Quick Send project introduced recipient-focused shortcuts to the Home screen, with the hypothesis that these shortcuts would particularly benefit our most active customers who often have multiple recipients they send to regularly.

Problem

Sending shortcuts to repeat similar transfers were effective, yet they were not modeled and placed in a way customers expected. Analysis revealed:

  • Users in the 11+ transfers state represented a $7.34M/quarter profit opportunity gap between average and top quartile profitability
  • 88% of top quartile users with 11+ transfers had more than 1 recipient (76% had >2, 64% had >3)
  • Research showed customers' mental model for sending was centered on recipients, not transfers or corridors
  • While 52% of users queued transfers to the same recipient, only 26% used 'Send Again' - especially pronounced with frequent senders
  • 19% of users queued to recipients that fell outside of recent transfer history (send again not available on home screen)

Goals

  • Increase next best action rate (send again) for frequent senders with multiple recipients
  • Improve send again order rate when no transfers are in flight, especially for Nth - No Active Txn (11+ txn) state customers
  • Match customers' recipient-first mental model for sending
  • Capture potential repeat transfers that fall outside of available transfer cards on the home screen

Process

Discovery & Analysis

Analyzed next best action rates across customer states and sending behavior to identify opportunities:

  • Conducted behavioral analysis showing that frequent senders (11+ transfers) had multiple recipients, yet 'Send Again' was underutilized
  • Reviewed data showing 52% of users queued transfers to the same recipient, but only 26% used 'Send Again'
  • Identified $7.34M/quarter profit opportunity gap for frequent senders
  • Validated through research that customers' mental model for sending is centered on recipients, not transfers

Design & Testing Strategy

Developed a multi-treatment experiment to test recipient-focused shortcuts:

  • Treatment 1: Recipient shortcuts shown above transfer cards
  • Treatment 2: Recipient shortcuts shown below transfer cards
  • Treatment 3: Recipient shortcuts shown above transfer cards with reduced transfer card count
  • Horizontal orientation to maximize recipients shown without excessive vertical space
  • Recipients ranked by most recent successful send, prioritizing current corridor

Validation

Ran a 14-day A/B test across app and web with multiple treatments, measuring revenue per user, order rate, and completion rate. Treatment 3 emerged as the winning variant.

Solution

The final design (Treatment 3) featured recipient-focused shortcuts prominently placed on the Home screen:

  • Recipient Quick Send Section: Horizontally scrollable list showing up to 10 recent recipients, ranked by most recent successful send
  • Recipient Cards: Each card displays recipient initials, name, delivery method, and last sent date
  • Strategic Placement: Positioned above transfer cards when no active transfers, with only 1 inactive transfer card shown to maximize Quick Send visibility
  • Smart Pre-population: Clicking a recipient opens send flow with all details from last transfer (recipient info, corridor, send amount, delivery method, payment method)
  • Cross-corridor Support (Web): Recipients from other corridors shown with country flag indicator, enabling seamless corridor switching
  • "Send to New Recipient" option: Quick access to add new recipients without leaving the home screen

Results

Treatment 3 (recipient shortcuts above 1 transfer card) showed statistically significant positive results:

  • +0.55% profit per user increase for frequent senders (Nth with 11+ transfers)
  • +0.66% completed transfers per user, indicating increased sending frequency
  • 51.9% feature interaction rate among exposed users, demonstrating strong adoption
  • $3.4M annualized profit opportunity from the feature improvement
  • Revenue per user stayed flat or increased across all test segments, validating the hypothesis
  • Strong performance particularly among users with multiple recipients, the primary target segment

Learnings

Key takeaways from this project:

  • Mental models matter: Aligning features with how users think (recipient-first vs. transfer-first) dramatically improves adoption and effectiveness
  • Small improvements, big impact: Even modest increases (+0.55% profit per user) can generate millions in annualized value when applied to the right customer segments
  • Placement is critical: Treatment 3 outperformed others by reducing visual competition—showing fewer transfer cards increased Quick Send visibility and usage
  • Test what matters: Multi-treatment testing revealed that not just the feature, but its positioning relative to existing elements, determined success
  • Data-driven segmentation: Targeting frequent senders with multiple recipients (88% of top quartile users) ensured we solved for the highest-value use case
  • Technical constraints shape design: Ranking by most recent send (vs. most frequent) was technically feasible and still delivered strong results, proving that perfect can be the enemy of good
