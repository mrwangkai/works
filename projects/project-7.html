Overview

The Plan is a mobile app concept designed to help Indiana University students navigate their academic journey. The project focused on simplifying course planning, schedule management, and degree progress tracking for undergraduate students.

As a student project, this work demonstrates early explorations in mobile UX design, user research, and interaction design principles applied to solving real student pain points.

Challenge

University students face complexity when planning their academic paths:

  • Course catalogs are difficult to navigate and understand prerequisite requirements
  • Planning multi-semester schedules requires juggling multiple systems and spreadsheets
  • Students struggle to visualize degree progress and remaining requirements
  • Existing university systems are fragmented and not mobile-friendly
  • Course scheduling conflicts and availability issues arise late in the registration process

Solution

The Plan provides an intuitive mobile interface for academic planning:

  • Visual Degree Progress: Clear visualization of completed, in-progress, and remaining degree requirements
  • Smart Course Search: Searchable course catalog with filters for requirements, time slots, and instructor ratings
  • Multi-Semester Planning: Drag-and-drop interface for planning future semesters with automatic prerequisite validation
  • Schedule Builder: Visual calendar view showing course times, locations, and potential conflicts
  • Registration Alerts: Notifications for course availability, registration deadlines, and schedule changes

Learnings

Key takeaways from this academic project:

  • User research foundations: Interviewing fellow students revealed pain points I hadn't personally experienced, emphasizing the importance of research over assumptions
  • Information architecture: Organizing complex academic requirements into digestible, scannable formats required multiple iterations and user testing
  • Mobile-first thinking: Designing for mobile forced prioritization of essential features and clear visual hierarchy
  • Interaction design patterns: Learned when to use familiar patterns versus custom solutions for unique problems
  • Iterative process: Early concepts needed significant refinement based on feedback and usability testing
